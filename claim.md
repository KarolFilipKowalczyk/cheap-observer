# The Claim

Sparks produce observers before they produce ruliads.

Take a simple rule — the kind you could write on a napkin — and give
it the smallest possible starting condition. Call the pair a spark.
Now run it. Let it rewrite, step after step, and watch what the
computation builds.

A spark is almost nothing. A rule and a seed. The cheapest possible
starting point for a computation. It has no memory, no goals, no
structure. It just runs.

An observer is a region of the computation that has an inside. It
persists over time. Its boundary holds roughly steady while its
interior keeps changing. Most of the information about its future
comes from its own past, not from the environment around it. And
its internal activity feeds back into itself — the output of its
dynamics re-enters its own spatial region. It does not need to be
aware. It does not need to understand anything. It just needs to be
a bounded region whose internal workings are partly its own business.

A ruliad is what happens when the computation becomes physics. The
causal structure stops depending on the order in which you apply the
rule. Different observers looking at the same evolution would agree
on what happened. There are stable laws, shared structure, effective
dimensionality. This is the regime that Wolfram's Physics Project
searches for. It is what we experience as an objective world.

We measure two things for each rule in a given class. First, the
earliest step at which an observer appears — the moment a region of
the computation first satisfies all four criteria simultaneously.
Second, the earliest step at which the computation exhibits
ruliad-like coherence — the moment it starts behaving like physics.
We call these T_obs and T_rul.

We then ask: across all rules in the class, which threshold is
reached first, and how often?

We expect to find that observers are common and physics is rare.
Most rules, given a minimal spark, will produce a region with an
inside long before they produce anything resembling shared,
law-governed dynamics. Among the rules that eventually produce both,
the observer almost always comes first. And these results hold even
when we tighten the definition of what counts as an observer, ruling
out borderline cases and verifying that random graph structure alone
cannot satisfy the criteria.

If this is correct, the default product of simple computation is not
a universe. It is a perspective. Interiority precedes objectivity.
